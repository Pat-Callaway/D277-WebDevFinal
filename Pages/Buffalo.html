A brief History

Buffalo, the second-largest city in New York State, has a rich history dating back to its founding in 1789. Initially a small trading community, Buffalo grew rapidly with the opening of the Erie Canal in 1825, becoming a critical hub for trade and transportation. The city played a significant role in the Underground Railroad, serving as a last stop before freedom in Canada for many escaped slaves. The late 19th and early 20th centuries saw Buffalo's golden age, with the Pan-American Exposition in 1901 highlighting its status as a center of industry and culture. However, the mid-20th century brought economic challenges, leading to a period of decline. In recent years, Buffalo has experienced a resurgence, with significant investment in the waterfront, healthcare, and education sectors. The city is known for its impressive architecture, including works by Frank Lloyd Wright and Louis Sullivan.
